Simple pendulum — equation of motion, period, and energy

Ideal model: point mass attached to a massless string, in a uniform gravitational field

The goal is to understand how a simple pendulum oscillates: why the period mainly depends on the length, when the small-angle approximation is valid, and how energy transforms during motion. In the simulation you will be able to change the length L, gravity g, and the initial angle.

Physical model and assumptions

  • The bob is a point mass with mass m.
  • Rigid, massless string of constant length L; frictionless pivot.
  • Motion takes place in a vertical plane; air resistance is neglected (ideal model).

The generalized coordinate is the angular displacement theta measured from the vertical.

Equation of motion

Along the tangential direction, the component of the weight is −m g sin(theta). Using Newton’s second law on a circular arc (tangential acceleration L * theta''):

$$m L\, \theta'' + m g \sin\theta = 0$$

Dividing by mL gives the nonlinear equation:

$$\theta'' + \dfrac{g}{L} \sin\theta = 0$$

Small-angle approximation

For small angles (in radians), sin(theta) ≈ theta. The equation becomes linear:

$$\theta'' + \dfrac{g}{L} \theta = 0$$

The solution is a simple harmonic oscillation:

$$\theta(t) = A \cos\!\left(\sqrt{\dfrac{g}{L}}\, t + \phi\right)$$

The oscillation period is (approximately) independent of amplitude:

$$T = 2\pi \sqrt{\dfrac{L}{g}}$$

Consequences: mass does not appear in the period; doubling L scales T by sqrt(2).

Real dependence on amplitude

At large angles the approximation sin(theta) ≈ theta breaks down. The period increases slightly with amplitude; a useful series (with theta0 in radians) is:

$$T \approx 2\pi\sqrt{\dfrac{L}{g}}\left[1 + \dfrac{1}{16}\theta_0^{2} + \dfrac{11}{3072}\theta_0^{4} + \cdots\right]$$

Energy

With no losses, mechanical energy is conserved; taking zero potential energy at the lowest point:

$$E = K + U = \tfrac{1}{2} m (L\,\theta')^{2} + m g L (1 - \cos\theta) = \text{const.}$$

At the bottom (theta = 0) energy is purely kinetic; at the turning point (theta = theta0) it is purely potential.

Damping (optional)

With weak drag (proportional to angular velocity), the equation becomes:

$$\theta'' + 2\beta\theta' + \dfrac{g}{L}\sin\theta = 0$$

For small angles and small beta, the amplitude decays exponentially while the period changes only slightly.

Measuring g with a pendulum

  1. Measure the length L precisely (pivot to bob center).
  2. Time N full oscillations (e.g., N ≥ 20), compute T_avg = time / N.
  3. Compute g = 4 pi^2 L / T_avg^2.

Typical errors: length measurement, too-large amplitude, air drag/torsion in the string.

Key takeaways

  • Exact equation: theta'' + (g/L) sin theta = 0; for small angles it becomes harmonic.
  • Ideal period: T = 2 pi sqrt(L/g) — independent of mass and (approximately) amplitude.
  • Energy shuttles between kinetic and potential; total is conserved in the ideal model.
