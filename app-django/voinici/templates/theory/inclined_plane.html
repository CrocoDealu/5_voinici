Inclined plane — forces, equilibrium, and motion

Theory material for the experiment with a cube resting on an inclined plane

The goal is to understand how a simple rigid body moves on an inclined plane. You will see the role of the plane angle, how the weight splits into useful components, what friction does, and under which conditions the body stays at rest or starts sliding.

Physical model

Consider a homogeneous cube on a rigid plane inclined at an angle θ to the horizontal. We neglect air resistance; contact is dry, characterized by a static friction coefficient μs and, once motion starts, a kinetic friction coefficient μk.

The forces are the weight G = mg, the normal reaction N, and the friction force F. The weight splits into two components: perpendicular to the plane, mg cos θ, and parallel to the plane, mg sin θ. The normal reaction is N = mg cos θ.

Equilibrium vs. onset of sliding

The body remains at rest as long as the parallel component of the weight is balanced by the maximum static friction:

mg sin θ ≤ μs mg cos θ → tan θ ≤ μs

When the angle increases so that tan θ > μs, static friction cannot balance the downslope component, and the body begins to slide. The critical angle is θcrit = arctan(μs).

Motion on the plane

After the body starts sliding, the net force along the plane is:

Fnet = mg sin θ − μk mg cos θ

Dividing by the mass gives the acceleration:

a = g (sin θ − μk cos θ)

Note the mass cancels — all bodies accelerate the same for the same angle and friction. With no friction, a = g sin θ.

Equations of motion

For a slide over distance d starting from rest:

v = √(2 a d), t = √(2 d / a)

Substituting a = g (sin θ − μk cos θ) lets you predict the time and final speed observed in the simulation.

Experimental determination of the coefficients

For μs, increase the angle slowly until the body just starts to slide; the measured critical angle gives μs = tan θcrit. For μk, pick an angle above the threshold, measure the time over a distance d, then compute the acceleration and back out μk via the formula above.

Energy viewpoint

Gravitational potential energy mgh is converted into work against friction and kinetic energy. With no friction:

½ m v² = m g d sin θ → v = √(2 g d sin θ)

Limiting cases

Horizontal plane (θ = 0): sin θ = 0, the body stays at rest. Near-vertical plane (θ → 90°): sin θ → 1, cos θ → 0, so a ≈ g. If μs is very large, the body can remain stuck up to very steep angles.

Conclusion

Motion on an inclined plane boils down to two key relations:

a = g (sin θ − μk cos θ)
tan θ > μs → sliding begins

These formulas enable a complete analysis and link angle, friction, speed, and time. In the simulator you can tune parameters and observe their direct effects.
